# 1) SODIUM DISORDERS

## 1. Definition / Introduction

* **Hyponatremia:** Na < 135 mmol/L. Risk: cerebral edema if rapid fall.
* **Hypernatremia:** Na > 145 mmol/L. Risk: cerebral shrinkage, hemorrhage if corrected too fast.

## 2. Etiology & Pathophysiology (short)

* **HypoNa:** Hypovolemic (GI losses, diuretics), Euvolemic (SIADH, hypothyroid, adrenal insufficiency), Hypervolemic (HF, cirrhosis, nephrosis).
* **HyperNa:** Free-water loss (diabetes insipidus, fever, osmotic diuresis) or sodium gain (rare; hypertonic infusions).

## 3. Clinical Signs

* Headache, nausea, confusion, seizures (both severe hypo/hyper).
* HyperNa often with lethargy, irritability, hyperreflexia.

## 4. Diagnostics

* Serum osm, urine osm, urine Na, glucose; assess volume status.
* **SIADH pattern:** low serum osm, **Uosm > 100 mOsm/kg**, **UNa > 30 mmol/L**.
* **HyperNa:** calculate free-water deficit.

## 5. Therapy

* **Severe symptomatic hypoNa (seizure, coma):** 3% NaCl **100 mL IV bolus**, repeat up to 2 more times while checking Na and symptoms.
* **Hypovolemic hypoNa:** 0.9% NaCl until euvolemic, then reassess.
* **SIADH:** fluid restriction (≤ 800–1000 mL/d), high-solute diet; in ICU crisis add hypertonic saline ± **desmopressin clamp** to avoid overcorrection.
* **Hypervolemic hypoNa:** loop diuretic + fluid/salt restriction; consider vasopressin antagonists in selected cases.
* **HyperNa:** Replace **free water** (enteral preferred; IV D5W if NPO). Aim correction ≤ **10 mmol/L per 24 h** (≤ 8 if chronic/at risk).
* **Rates:** For chronic hypoNa, limit to **≤ 8–10 mmol/L in 24 h**, **≤ 18 in 48 h** to prevent osmotic demyelination.

## 6. Complications & Prognosis

* Rapid overcorrection of hypoNa → **osmotic demyelination**.
* Over-rapid hyperNa correction → **cerebral edema**.

## 7. MNEMONICS & TIPS

* **“SALT-RESCUE”** in severe SIADH: **S**aline 3%, **A**void rapid rise, **L**oop if hypervolemic, **T**rack Na q2–4h, **RE**strain fluids, **S**et target rise, **C**lamp with DDAVP if Na jumps, **U**rine osm/Na guide, **E**xclude endocrine (TSH, cortisol).
* **CHECK GLUCOSE:** Correct Na for hyperglycemia.

## 8. Clinical Example

A 68-year-old with pneumonia, Na 112, confusion, Uosm 550, UNa 45 → severe symptomatic SIADH. Give **3% NaCl 100 mL IV**; reassess. Restrict fluids, treat pneumonia. If Na rises too fast, add **DDAVP** and free water to re-lower toward target.

## 9. Final 3 to Remember

* Correct **slowly** unless actively seizing.
* Use **Uosm/UNa** to classify hypoNa.
* **Desmopressin clamp** prevents overshoot in SIADH.

# 2) POTASSIUM DISORDERS

## 1. Definition

* **Hypokalemia:** K < 3.5 mmol/L; **Hyperkalemia:** K > 5.0 mmol/L.

## 2. Etiology & Pathophysiology

* **HypoK:** diuretics, GI loss, insulin/β-agonists, hyperaldosteronism; often **Mg deficiency**.
* **HyperK:** renal failure, RAAS blockers, K-sparing diuretics, tissue breakdown, acidosis, hemolysis.

## 3. Clinical Signs

* Weakness, cramps, ileus (hypoK); paresthesias, weakness (hyperK).
* **Arrhythmia risk** both ends.

## 4. Diagnostics

* ECG; Mg level; acid–base; hemolysis check (repeat plasma K if suspicious).

## 5. Therapy

* **HyperK (emergency if ECG changes or K ≥ 6.0):**

  1. **Stabilize membrane:** **Calcium gluconate 1 g IV** over 5–10 min; repeat if ECG unchanged.
  2. **Shift K intracellular:** **Regular insulin 10 U IV + 25 g dextrose**, **nebulized albuterol 10–20 mg**, **NaHCO₃** if severe acidosis.
  3. **Remove K:** **loop diuretics**, **sodium zirconium cyclosilicate** or **patiromer** (not for emergencies), **hemodialysis** if refractory/renal failure.
* **HypoK:**

  * Oral KCl preferred if mild/moderate.
  * **IV KCl** if K < 3.0, symptomatic, or NPO: typically **10 mEq/h peripheral**, up to **20 mEq/h central** with monitoring.
  * **Replete Mg** (e.g., **MgSO₄ 2 g IV**) to fix refractory hypoK.

## 6. Complications

* Ventricular arrhythmias, cardiac arrest; rhabdomyolysis (hypoK).

## 7. MNEMONICS & TIPS

* **“C-BIG K DROP”** for hyperK: **C**alcium, **B**icarbonate (if acidotic), **I**nsulin, **G**lucose, **K**β-agonist, **D**iuretics/**R**esins/**O**utpatient binders/**P**Dialysis.
* **CHECK Mg** in every difficult hypokalemia.

## 8. Clinical Example

K 6.8 with peaked T waves: give **1 g Ca-gluconate IV**, **10 U insulin + 25 g dextrose**, **albuterol**, start **loop diuretic**; call dialysis if CKD5 and anuric.

## 9. Final 3 to Remember

* Treat **ECG first** in hyperK.
* **Shift then remove** potassium.
* HypoK often needs **magnesium**.

# 3) CALCIUM DISORDERS

## 1. Definition

* Correct for albumin or measure ionized calcium.

## 2. Etiology & Pathophysiology

* **HypoCa:** hypoparathyroid, CKD, vitamin D deficiency, pancreatitis, citrate load (massive transfusion), sepsis; worsened by hypoMg.
* **HyperCa:** hyperparathyroidism, malignancy (PTHrP, bone mets), vitamin D excess, granulomatous disease, thiazides.

## 3. Clinical Signs

* **HypoCa:** perioral numbness, cramps, **Chvostek/Trousseau**, seizures, QT **prolongation**.
* **HyperCa:** polyuria, constipation, abdominal pain, confusion, **short QT**, nephrolithiasis.

## 4. Diagnostics

* Ionized Ca, albumin, PTH, vitamin D, Mg, phosphate, ECG.

## 5. Therapy

* **Symptomatic hypoCa:** **Calcium gluconate 1–2 g IV** slowly, telemetry; correct **Mg**.
* **Severe hyperCa (≥ 14 or symptomatic):**

  * **Isotonic saline** to euvolemia.
  * **Calcitonin** for rapid, short-term drop.
  * **IV bisphosphonate** (e.g., zoledronic acid) for sustained control; **denosumab** if renal failure or refractory.
  * Dialysis in malignant, refractory, or renal failure cases.

## 6. Complications

* Arrhythmias, seizures (hypoCa); AKI, coma (hyperCa).

## 7. MNEMONICS & TIPS

* **“FLUIDS FIRST”** for hyperCa.
* **“QT tells you Ca”**: long = low, short = high.

## 8. Clinical Example

Metastatic breast cancer, Ca 15, confusion: start **NS**, give **calcitonin**, **zoledronic acid**; monitor urine output; stop thiazide.

## 9. Final 3 to Remember

* Use **ionized Ca** for accuracy.
* HyperCa: **saline → calcitonin → bisphosphonate**.
* HypoCa often needs **Mg repletion**.

# 4) MAGNESIUM DISORDERS

## 1. Definition

* **Hypomagnesemia:** Mg < 0.7 mmol/L; **Hypermagnesemia:** rare, usually iatrogenic/renal failure.

## 2. Etiology & Pathophysiology

* **HypoMg:** diuretics, PPIs, diarrhea, alcoholism, cisplatin.
* **HyperMg:** Mg-containing antacids/laxatives in CKD.

## 3. Clinical Signs

* **HypoMg:** tremor, tetany, seizures, refractory hypoK/hypoCa, torsades (QT prolongation).
* **HyperMg:** hyporeflexia, hypotension, bradycardia, respiratory depression.

## 4. Diagnostics

* ECG, Ca, K. Consider urinary Mg if unclear.

## 5. Therapy

* **Symptomatic or torsades:** **MgSO₄ 2 g IV** over 5–10 min, then infusion.
* **Asymptomatic hypoMg:** **1–2 g IV** q6–8h or oral Mg oxide if tolerated.
* **HyperMg:** stop Mg, IV fluids + loop diuretics; **calcium gluconate** for cardiac protection; dialysis if severe CKD.

## 6. Complications

* Ventricular arrhythmias (low); cardio-respiratory collapse (very high).

## 7. MNEMONICS & TIPS

* **“LOW Mg = LOW K & LOW Ca”** unless you fix Mg first.

## 8. Clinical Example

Alcohol withdrawal patient with torsades: give **2 g MgSO₄ IV** STAT and monitor.

## 9. Final 3 to Remember

* Think Mg in **torsades** and refractory **hypoK/hypoCa**.
* Replace IV if symptomatic.
* Protect heart with **calcium** in hyperMg.

# 5) PHOSPHATE DISORDERS

## 1. Definition

* **Hypophosphatemia:** < 0.8 mmol/L; **severe** < 0.32.
* **Hyperphosphatemia:** usually CKD, tumor lysis, rhabdo.

## 2. Etiology & Pathophysiology

* **HypoPhos:** refeeding, DKA treatment (insulin shifts), alcohol use, antacids, respiratory alkalosis.
* Low phosphate → decreased ATP, 2,3-DPG → tissue hypoxia, weakness, respiratory failure.

## 3. Clinical Signs

* Myopathy, paresthesia, hemolysis, rhabdomyolysis, decreased diaphragmatic strength.

## 4. Diagnostics

* Check Ca (risk of Ca-Phos precipitation), Mg, renal function.

## 5. Therapy

* **Severe or symptomatic:** **IV Na- or K-phosphate 15–30 mmol** over 2–6 h (choose **K-phosphate if K low**, **Na-phosphate if K high**).
* **Mild/moderate:** oral phosphate 250–500 mg PO TID.
* **HyperPhos:** dietary restriction, **phosphate binders**, dialysis if severe CKD.

## 6. Complications

* Ca-Phos precipitation, AKI; respiratory failure if severe low Phos.

## 7. MNEMONICS & TIPS

* **“PHOS = POWER”**: low phosphate = low ATP → weakness, vent failure.

## 8. Clinical Example

Refeeding after malnutrition, Phos 0.25 mmol/L: give **IV K-phos** with telemetry, slow feeds, thiamine.

## 9. Final 3 to Remember

* Severe hypoPhos can **paralyze respiration**.
* Pick **Na- vs K-phos** based on serum K.
* Watch **Ca-Phos product**.

# 6) CHLORIDE & ACID–BASE

## 1. Definition

* Chloride tracks sodium and acid–base; low Cl hints **metabolic alkalosis**, high Cl suggests **non-gap acidosis**.

## 2. Etiology & Pathophysiology

* **Metabolic alkalosis:** vomiting/NG suction, diuretics → **Cl-responsive**; mineralocorticoid excess → **Cl-resistant**.
* **Hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis:** diarrhea, renal tubular acidosis, saline overload.

## 3. Clinical Signs

* Volume status clues, K abnormalities, neurologic symptoms from pH shifts.

## 4. Diagnostics

* ABG/VBG, **urine chloride** (low in Cl-responsive alkalosis), anion gap, Δ-Δ.

## 5. Therapy

* **Cl-responsive alkalosis:** **0.9% NaCl** and **K repletion**.
* **Hyperchloremic acidosis:** treat cause; consider balanced crystalloids.

## 6–9. Key Points

* Use **urine Cl** to classify alkalosis.
* Prefer **balanced crystalloids** to avoid iatrogenic hyperchloremic acidosis.
* Always integrate **acid–base** with electrolytes.

# Practical Inpatient Replacement Targets

* **Potassium:** aim 4.0–4.5 in cardiac patients; IV **10 mEq/h** peripheral, **20 mEq/h** central with monitoring.
* **Magnesium:** aim ≥ 0.8 mmol/L in arrhythmia-prone; **2 g IV** for torsades/symptoms.
* **Phosphate:** replete if < 0.8 mmol/L; aggressively if < 0.32 or ventilated.
* **Sodium:** set **daily correction goal** and **check q2–4h** in active treatment.

---

# “WHAT TO DO FIRST?” ALGORITHMS

**A. Hyponatremia with seizures:**

1. **3% NaCl 100 mL IV** now; repeat if needed.
2. Check Na q2h; set target rise 4–6 mmol first 6 h.
3. Add **DDAVP** if Na rising too fast.

**B. Hyperkalemia ≥ 6.0 or ECG changes:**

1. **Calcium gluconate 1 g IV.**
2. **Insulin 10 U IV + 25 g dextrose**, **albuterol neb**.
3. **Loop diuretic / binder / dialysis**; re-check K in 1 h.

**C. Severe hypercalcemia (≥ 14):**

1. **NS resuscitation.**
2. **Calcitonin**.
3. **Zoledronic acid**; consider **denosumab** if CKD.

**D. Severe hypophosphatemia (< 0.32):**

1. **IV phosphate 15–30 mmol**; choose Na vs K salt.
2. Slow feeds if refeeding; add thiamine.

# QUICK CLINICAL SCENARIOS

1. **Post-op patient on thiazides with weakness, QT long:** K 2.8, Mg 0.5.

   * **Tx:** IV **MgSO₄ 2 g**, then **KCl**; telemetry.

2. **DKA on insulin infusion; new weakness, low Phos:**

   * **Tx:** **IV K-phos** (if K not high), monitor Ca.

3. **Cirrhosis with edema, Na 121, Uosm 600, UNa 50 (SIADH-like physiology):**

   * **Tx:** Restrict fluids, treat trigger, consider loop + salt tabs; avoid rapid correction.

4. **Anuric CKD with K 7.2, wide QRS:**

   * **Tx:** **Calcium**, **insulin + dextrose**, **albuterol**, **emergent dialysis**.

---

# FINAL CHECK: 3 BIG TAKEAWAYS

1. **Stabilize first**: ECG and neuro symptoms trump numbers.
2. **Correct at safe rates**: Na ≤ 8–10 mmol/day; monitor closely.
3. **Think pairs**: HypoK ↔ hypoMg; Ca ↔ phosphate; chloride ↔ acid–base.
